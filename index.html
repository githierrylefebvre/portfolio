2025

Virtual twin

This video showcases a digital twin of the city of Rennes (WebGL), based on technology similar to Cesium 3DTiles ...

This scene is composed of terrain, buildings, vegetation, and urban furniture. The terrain utilizes the TIN format (detailed in the section below) and is streamed via a protocol similar to 3D Tiles (using a quadtree spatial structure). The buildings are derived from CityGML data, converted into 3D meshes with multiple Levels of Detail (LOD). Vegetation and urban furniture are sourced from GeoJSON and Shapefiles. Due to their heterogeneous spatial distribution and repetitive nature, these elements are managed using a KD-tree to balance the spatial structure nodes. Finally, hardware instancing is utilized to optimize both the loading and rendering performance of these assets.

2024

TIN Terrain

I worked on TIN (Triangulated Irregular Network) terrain surface modeling...

The algorithm computes terrain tiles from GeoTIFF files containing elevation data. It is an iterative process that progressively adds the most significant points—those that minimize surface error—until a specific quality threshold or a maximum vertex count is reached. This results in an adaptive tessellation of the terrain based on its topography, as demonstrated in the video (featuring the terrain of Réunion Island).

2020

Al'Ula

Here is a 3D representation of the historic village of Al-Ula in Saudi Arabia...

Starting from LiDAR data containing several hundred million triangles, the objective was to load the entire dataset and enable real-time navigation within a 3D web application. To achieve this, I implemented a spatial structure (an octree) for progressive loading. Multiple Levels of Detail (LOD) were generated using decimation algorithms (vertex clustering). Additionally, it was necessary to implement mesh stitching between blocks to close the gaps introduced during the decimation process.

2014

WebGL earth

This project involved modeling the Earth in WebGL at a 1:1 scale...

This required addressing precision issues commonly encountered when handling large geometric coordinates on the GPU. The planet's surface is represented by a quadtree that refines as the camera approaches the surface. Elevation data (vertex displacement) is applied to represent the terrain's topography, along with an ortho-imagery layer. To enhance visual realism, an atmospheric scattering algorithm was implemented in GLSL, coupled with a dynamic cloud layer. Finally, the sun's position is calculated based on the date and time to manage day/night cycles.

2010

Realistic Globe / Ocean

Within the context of a realistic globe implemented in C# / SharpDX, I developed a real-time ocean rendering system...

The ocean movement is represented by periodic trochoidal functions, which allow for the superposition of motion across different scales and frequencies while ensuring seamless continuity across the entire surface. The rendering, implemented in HLSL, uses a lighting model that incorporates transparency, specular highlights, Image-Based Lighting (IBL), and the Fresnel effect.

2004-2007

Temporal aliasing

Another topic I addressed is temporal aliasing : when displaying images at a specific frequency, each frame only represents a snapshot of a single, precise moment in time...

However, time elapses between each frame, making judder and stuttering effects particularly visible on immersive cylindrical screens. To resolve this, I implemented post-process rendering techniques designed to integrate the displacement between two successive frames for every pixel. This not only reduced visual artifacts but also drastically changed the drivers' perception of speed within the simulator. Ultimately, this allows for the study of driver behavior based on their dynamic perception of the 3D scene.

2004-2007

Glare simulation

In addition to lighting distribution, I also focused on glare simulation to assess driver visibility...

Indeed, because the limited luminous power of video projectors prevents the human eye from reacting as it would in a natural environment, simulating its behavior becomes essential. To address this, I developed a glare module that simulates the human eye's response to intense light sources. This allows for the study of driver behavior in such conditions within a driving simulator. These applications also extend to tunnel entries and exits, where abrupt lighting changes can alter human perception and compromise road safety.

2004-2007

Lighting Simulation

During my PhD, I specialized in lighting simulation for driving simulators. The ultimate goal was to validate the photometric distribution of optical units using numerical simulations alone, eliminating the need for physical prototypes...

In this context, I developed real-time methods to calculate material reflectance, achieving images that are both visually realistic and physically accurate for precise lighting visualization and measurement. These rendering techniques involve complex material representations (measured BRDFs, synthesized BTFs), mathematical tools for modeling light functions, and the implementation of these models on the GPU.
